Nutrition During Cancer Treatment

Practical, gentle guidance to support energy, immunity and comfort

Quick Principles

Nutrition needs often change during treatment. The goals are to maintain strength, preserve lean body mass, prevent or treat weight loss, and keep you as comfortable as possible.

Calories

Eat frequent small meals or snacks if appetite is low. Choose energy-dense foods if weight gain is needed.

Protein

Prioritise protein at each meal (eggs, dairy, legumes, fish, poultry, tofu, protein shakes) to help healing and preserve muscle.

Hydration

Sip fluids regularly — water, milk, oral rehydration, broths; treat mouth sores and nausea with cool drinks or ice chips.

Food Safety

When white blood cell counts are low, avoid raw/undercooked eggs, seafood, unpasteurised dairy, and unwashed produce.

Manage Common Eating Problems

Nausea

  • Small bland meals, dry crackers before getting up, avoid strong smells.
  • Cool foods are often better than hot ones. Ginger (tea, chews) and mint may help.
  • Take anti-nausea meds as prescribed before meals when advised by your team.

Mouth sores & taste changes

  • Soft, moist, cool foods (yogurt, smoothies, mashed potatoes, well-cooked cereals) are gentler.
  • Avoid spicy, acidic, or very salty foods if they sting. Try plastic cutlery if metallic taste is a problem.
  • Frequent mouth rinses (salt-soda) as recommended by dental/oncology team.

Loss of appetite / weight loss

  • Eat calorie-dense snacks: nut butters, full-fat dairy, smoothies with milk and protein powder, avocados.
  • Try liquid nutrition supplements if eating is difficult — ask your team for brand/type guidance.
  • Focus on protein first if you can't finish a meal — a small portion of meat/legume/egg is better than none.

Sample Meal Plan Ideas

Breakfast

  • Oat porridge made with milk, mashed banana, nut butter and cinnamon
  • Boiled egg or yogurt on the side

Lunch

  • Chicken or lentil soup with soft vegetables, rice or bread
  • Avocado on toast or a side of cottage cheese

Snack

  • Smoothie: milk or plant milk + protein powder + fruit + nut butter
  • Crackers with hummus, cheese or nut butter

Dinner

  • Baked fish or tofu, mashed potato or soft rice, cooked vegetables
  • Yogurt with honey for dessert

Cancer Treatment Side Effect Manager

Practical advice and quick resources for cancer treatment side effects

Overview

This page helps patients and caregivers understand common side effects from cancer treatments (chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immunotherapy, surgery and targeted therapies) and provides practical, evidence-based tips you can share with your care team.

Common Treatments

Chemotherapy nausea, fatigue, mouth sores, low blood counts, hair loss
Radiotherapy skin changes, tiredness, local hair loss
Immunotherapy immune-related inflammation (e.g., skin, gut, lungs)
Targeted therapy rash, blood pressure changes
Surgery pain, wound healing, mobility changes

Key Principles

  • Report new or worsening symptoms to your care team quickly.
  • Preventive measures (anti-nausea meds, mouth care, skin care) help—ask before symptoms start.
  • Keep a symptom diary to share with your doctor.

Practical Tips by Side Effect

Nausea & vomiting

Take anti-nausea medicines as prescribed, try small bland meals, stay hydrated and avoid strong smells.

Fatigue

Pace yourself, prioritise important tasks, consider light activity as tolerated and speak with your team about anemia or sleep problems.

Mouth sores & dry mouth

Use gentle mouth rinses (salt/soda rinse), avoid alcohol-based mouthwash, maintain oral hygiene and see dental support if severe.

Low blood counts (infection risk, bleeding)

Avoid crowds if neutropenic, watch for fever, report any bleeding, and follow instructions about vaccines and infection prevention.

Skin changes

Use mild soap, moisturiser, gentle clothing; protect treated skin from sun and follow radiotherapy skin-care instructions from your team.

When to Call Your Care Team

Call right away for:

fever (over 100.4°F/38°C), uncontrolled bleeding, severe shortness of breath, severe pain not controlled by medication, sudden confusion or signs of severe infection.

Keep your care team phone numbers and after-hours instructions handy so you can act fast.
